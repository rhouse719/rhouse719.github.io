The Slander of Richard Stallman

Messages from an MIT mailing list sent between 2019-09-10 and 2019-09-12 were leaked. In these messages, Stallman made several comments and arguments about his long time friend Marvin Minsky, who was involved in the Epstein scandal. This was followed by several slanderous articles about those comments, which misrepresented what Stallman said.

The Vice Article

The Vice article linked above, written by pretend journalist Edward Ongweso Jr, contains several lies about Stallman's statements. The first is this:

> Early in the thread, Stallman insists that the “most plausible scenario” is that Epstein’s underage victims were “entirely willing” while being trafficked.

This is a clear misrepresentation of what Stallman actually said in the email thread:

> We can imagine many scenarios, but the most plausible scenario is that she presented herself to him as entirely willing. Assuming she was being coerced by Epstein, he would have had every reason to tell her to conceal that from most of his associates.

There are two key differences:

  1. Stallman is talking about Giuffre in particular, but Ongweso claimed that Stallman was talking about "Epstein's underage victims." Notice the "s" on "victims."
  2. Stallman never said that Giuffre was , just that she presented herself as willing. These are two very different statements.

It's always a red flag when the writer clipping very small quotes out of context like this. If he wanted his audience to actually know what Stallman said, he'd just quote the entire paragraph as I did above. Instead he writes his own narrative around two out of context quotes to make it seem like Stallman said something he didn't. Ongweso's next misrepresentation was as follows:

> Stallman goes on to argue about the definition of “sexual assault,” “rape,” and whether they apply to Minsky and Giuffre’s deposition statement that she was forced to have sex with him.

Again Ongweso has misrepresented Stallman's actual statement. Stallman's statement about the term "sexual assault":

> The injustice is in the word "assaulting". The term "sexual assault" is so vague and slipper that it facilitates accusation inflation: taking claims that someone did X and leading people to think of it as Y, which is much worse than X.

As the first statement from Stallman makes obvious, he wasn't arguing about whether the term assault, by its legal definition, applies to this case. He is arguing that it is irresponsible to use the term because it invokes in a reader a very different event from what actually took place. He makes that quite obvious here: "taking claims that someone did X and leading people to think of it as Y, which is much worse than X." Ongweso's claim is basically accurate with regards to Stallman's statements about the term "rape":

> Does it really? I think it is morally absurd to define "rape" in a way that depends on minor details such as which country it was in or whether the victim was 18 years old or 17.

From Sylvia Paull's Medium

> There is no single person that is so deserving of praise their comments deprecating others should be allowed to slide. Particularly when those comments are excuses about rape, assault, and child sex trafficking.

Except that's not true. Stallman didn't excuse "rape, assault, and child sex trafficking." He never argues with the claims being made against Minsky by Giuffre. He only states that the terms used by some to describe Minsky's actions are misleading to people reading them. Which is potentially true. I can't claim to have read any studies on the matter, but I could easily see how the use of terms like "assault" and "rape" could lead a reader to the belief that something far more heinous took place. It seems to me that Stallman was simply suggesting that when describing the accusations against Minsky, one ought to be more specific and to not use easily misunderstood terms like "assault" and "rape."
